# Pricing Strategy

Develop pricing strategies that capture value, drive growth, and align with customer willingness to pay.

## When to Use
- Launching a new product
- Repricing existing offerings
- Adding/changing tiers
- Moving to usage-based pricing
- Entering new markets

## Don't Use When
- You're copying competitors without research
- You haven't validated customer value
- You're changing prices reactively without strategy

## Pricing Models

### 1. Tiered Pricing (Good-Better-Best)
**Best for:** Most SaaS, clear feature differentiation

**Structure:**
- **Starter:** Entry point, limited features
- **Professional:** Most popular, full features
- **Enterprise:** Custom, high-touch support

**Best practices:**
- 3 tiers maximum (reduces decision paralysis)
- Highlight "most popular" tier
- Clear upgrade path between tiers
- Price anchoring (enterprise tier makes others look cheap)

### 2. Usage-Based Pricing
**Best for:** Infrastructure, APIs, variable usage

**Types:**
- Per-seat (team tools)
- Per-usage (API calls, storage, bandwidth)
- Per-outcome (commission, success-based)

**Considerations:**
- Predictability vs. flexibility tradeoff
- Customer preference for predictable bills
- Revenue volatility

### 3. Freemium
**Best for:** Network effects, viral growth, low marginal cost

**Structure:**
- Free tier: Core value, clear limitations
- Paid tiers: Advanced features, higher limits, support

**Success factors:**
- Free tier must deliver real value
- Clear upgrade triggers
- Time/usage limits that drive conversion

### 4. Value-Based Pricing
**Best for:** High-value B2B, ROI is clear

**Approach:**
- Price as % of value created
- Customer-specific pricing
- Requires discovery and sales process

## Pricing Psychology

### Anchoring
- Show highest price first
- Include a "decoy" tier
- "Normally $X, now $Y"

### Charm Pricing
- $99 vs $100 (perception of being in lower tier)
- Works better for B2C and self-serve

### Decoy Effect
- Add a tier that's clearly worse to make target tier look better
- Example: Basic ($29), Pro ($99), Premium ($109) → Pro looks like a deal

### Bundle Pricing
- Combine products for perceived discount
- Increases average order value

## Pricing Research Methods

### 1. Van Westendorp Price Sensitivity Meter
**Questions:**
- At what price would you consider it too expensive?
- At what price would you consider it too cheap?
- At what price would it start to seem expensive?
- At what price would it be a bargain?

**Output:** Acceptable price range

### 2. Conjoint Analysis
**Best for:** Understanding feature/value tradeoffs

**Approach:** Present different feature/price combinations, measure preference

### 3. Direct Customer Interviews
**Questions:**
- What would this be worth to you?
- What would you pay for this outcome?
- How do you currently budget for this?

## Pricing Page Best Practices

- Lead with annual (but show monthly toggle)
- Show savings for annual
- Include all features in each tier (not just "checkmarks")
- Add FAQ section
- Offer free trial or demo CTA
- Show trusted payment security

## Common Pricing Mistakes

1. **Pricing too low** — Hard to raise, attracts wrong customers
2. **No annual option** — Missed cash flow and retention
3. **Too many tiers** — Decision paralysis
4. **Hidden pricing** — Friction for self-serve buyers
5. **No upgrade path** — Trapped revenue
